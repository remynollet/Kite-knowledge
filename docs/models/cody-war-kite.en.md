# Cody War Kite

## Summary

The **Cody War Kite** represents the pinnacle of the kite as a tool for war and observation. Developed between 1901 and 1903 by inventor **Samuel Franklin Cody**, this kite was a major evolution of Lawrence Hargrave's cellular structure. Its distinctiveness lay in the addition of large lateral wings (ailerons) that significantly increased **lift** and ensured exceptional lateral stability, even in high winds.

Cody's system did not use a single kite, but a "train" of kites. Several large models were launched in series on a single line to achieve a traction force capable of lifting a wicker basket containing a man. This system was safer and more discreet than the captive balloons of the time, as it carried no risk of explosion and was less sensitive to enemy fire.

## Historical Context

The Cody War Kite was officially adopted by the British Admiralty and the War Office. In 1903, Cody proved the efficiency of his system by crossing the English Channel in a boat towed by one of his kites. The British Army used the "War Kites" during naval maneuvers to spot long-range artillery fire.

The structure of the War Kite, made of cotton canvas and **spars** of bamboo or light wood, was remarkably robust. This experience with **lift** and directional control allowed Cody to design his first gliders, and later his motorized aircraft. Today, the Cody War Kite remains a construction challenge for kite flyers passionate about history, symbolizing the heroic transition from traditional kites to modern aeronautics.
